## I. Math

**Event algebra**
: Boolean algebra of events.  A two valued algebra of True and False. (French, Ch. 6)

<span style="color:rgb(146, 208, 80)">Rules of actional thought</span>
: Five rules derived from the axioms of choice
- **probability** you use possibilities and probabilities to provide the distinctions and information that characterize deals.
- **order** You can arrange prospects in a list in descending order of your preference from best to worst.
- **equivalence** you can specify a number p so that you will be indifferent between receiving prospect B for sure and receiving a deal with probability p of prospect A and probability 1-p of prospect C where A > B> C. 
- **substitution** The substitution rule requires that you be indifferent between receiving any prospect and any deal for which you have established that prospect as a certain equivalent.
- **choice**. The rule says that you must choose the deal with the higher probability of the prospect you like better.

#### Utility

<span style="color:rgb(146, 208, 80)">preference probability</span>
: Probability assigned to a certain outcome (a prospect). A consequence of the equivalence rule - the utility of a prospect. There is both a "preference probability of a prospect" -- a utility,  and the "preference probability of an alternative" -- an e-value of the prospects' utilities. 
Preference is quantified by a utility function, which can express time and risk preference to the value of an outcome.  
Preference probabilities are a consequence of the *equivalence rule.*

<span style="color:rgb(146, 208, 80)">u-curve</span>
: A **Utility function** that maps e.g. dollars into preference probabilities (utilities on a scale set by the min and max prospects used in elicitation.)

<span style="color:rgb(146, 208, 80)">e-value</span>  (equivalent value)
: a probability weighted sum of preference probabilities, the consequence of an "e-operation" -- an **expected value,** by taking an **expectation**.  Also in probability terms the *mean value* of the distribution. The "e-value"  of a deal with a u-curve is a <span style="color:rgb(146, 208, 80)">u-value</span>, e.g. the "e-value of a u-value."

<span style="color:rgb(146, 208, 80)">delta property</span>
: a Utility function that has constant risk aversion for changes in wealth.

<span style="color:rgb(146, 208, 80)">risk odds</span>
:  Given an exponential utility function, the ratio $r_m = \frac{p}{1-p}$ where $p$ is the preference probability for a reward of m, versus a loss of m. $r$ is the base to the exponent in the utility function $u(x) = a - b r^{-x}$ .  When risk neutral, this ratio is equal to 1 and the function reduces to $u(x) = a + bx$.  

* risk aversion coefficient $\gamma$  ( p 231)
* risk tolerance coefficient $\rho$

<span style="color:rgb(146, 208, 80)">certain equivalent</span>
: the PISP, possibly for an uncertain deal. Computed by taking the inverse of the utility function.

<span style="color:rgb(146, 208, 80)">the mutual property</span> ( p 125)
: that relevance is non-directional ( "mutual"),  unlike cause that is directional. This is called the "associative vs distinctive logic"

Cause
: The means by which we understand change. An explanation is just a description of a cause.  This is a case where the decision analysis community invented euphemisms -- "influence",  "relevance" -- to avoid using the obvious term.  Precisely a cause is defined by a hypothetical -- knowing what would be the difference in the consequence should the event in question not have occured -- the "counterfactual."  Resolving whether a cause exists or not is the point of scientific method, in those cases where cause is not apparent.
