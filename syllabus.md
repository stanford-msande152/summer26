**MS&E 152: Introduction to Decision Analysis** · Stanford University · Summer 2026 · 4 units

## Course Description

Decision Analysis is an application of Decision Theory to making good decisions when outcomes are uncertain. It applies to practical problems in engineering, organizations, and even one's personal life. It is a principled, quantitative approach that draws upon theory in Economics, Applied Probability, and Psychology. These principles of rational choice extend to applications that combine individual judgment with quantitative, data-driven modeling techniques.

This course will develop skills in structuring and solving problems through interactive and project class work. By working with probability and value models, students will gain an understanding of how to quantify belief, express risk and time preference, and how information can be valued. An introduction to Influence Diagrams will address how decisions are affected by how a problem is framed, illustrated by cases from finance, project management, law, and medicine. In conclusion we will touch on issues of Decision Quality, the limits of rationality, and the ethical implications of a decision.

## Prerequisites

As this is an Engineering course, ability in high-school level mathematics and spreadsheets is needed. A background in probability is helpful. Co-registration in an introductory probability course is appropriate for students with no previous study. Basic software or programming (e.g. Python notebooks) experience is entirely optional.

#### Pre-course assignment

- Review your understanding of basic probability with the provided review exercise (on Canvas).
- Watch [this TED talk](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-gqNOmdvwiI) on the value of improving personal decision making.

## Learning Objectives

By virtue of taking this course you should:

1. Understand how the fundamentals of rational choice lead to the definition of a good decision, as distinct from its eventual outcome.
2. Be able to apply the probability "calculus" by which belief can be elicited and uncertainty quantified.
3. Know how to formulate a decision model that integrates decisions, probabilities, and values.
4. Be able to compute how a decision model can determine whether additional information is worth acquiring.
5. Be skilled in how one can assist others by the process of Decision Analysis and application of the principles of Decision Quality.

The course runs for eight weeks, with two lecture sessions per week.

- **Lectures:** Mon/Wed, 10:30 AM–12:30 PM · [Y2E2 111](http://campus-map.stanford.edu/?srch=Y2E2+111) · Jun 22 – Aug 13, 2026
- **Discussion Section:** Thu, 3:00–4:15 PM · [Y2E2 111](http://campus-map.stanford.edu/?srch=Y2E2+111)

Lectures and discussion sections will not be recorded. Lecture notes will be posted on the course website after each session.

The best way to communicate is in person, after lecture or during office hours — real-time meetings are more efficient, more effective, and more fun. Bring your laptop to discussion sections! You will need to attend some discussion sessions where you will be asked to explain how you solved one of your previous homework problems. We are also available by appointment.

**Online Discussion:** All online forum discussions take place using **Ed Discussion**. Please use it as the exclusive online tool to ask questions, discuss topics of common interest, and to form project teams. "Third channel" media that do not include all students are unfair to those not included.

**DO NOT** register for this course if you have a recurring conflict with the lecture times.

**Grading Exceptions**

*Re-assigning points:* Students may assign points from missed homeworks or quizzes to other assignments. For example, a missed quiz may increase the midterm percentage by 2 points. Email us your re-assignment choice no later than when the assignment is due. Midterm and project points may not be re-assigned.

*Slip days:* Three slip days are available for late homework, with no grade penalty. For example, one homework two days late and another one day late — no penalty.

## Assignments and Exams

**Homework** is assigned weekly on Wednesdays, due the following Monday. It is graded on a completion basis — full score for an honest effort, partial credit for incomplete submissions. You will be asked to come to office hours at least twice during the quarter to review your homework answers verbally.

*Collaboration:* Collaboration is encouraged. However, the homework you submit must be your own work. Under the Stanford Honor Code, you are allowed to collaborate with each other and with AI. We ask that you:
- Write the names of your study group on your homework.
- List your sources: texts, notes, web pages, or AI models and how you used them (include a summary of your prompt and the AI's response).
- Write up your homework yourself — all submitted text must be typed or hand-written by you.
- Submit as a file on Canvas. Do not seek out or share solutions from previous years.

**Quizzes** are a one-question "pop" quiz at the start of each class, covering previous material. Attendance is recorded via quizzes and Poll Everywhere.

**Midterm** is in class (Jul 22), using novel probability-based scoring drawing on course concepts. You are allowed one hand-written reference sheet. Closed book, no electronic devices.

**Group Project** is mandatory. Groups of 2–4 students explore a situation that can be modeled as a decision. Each group presents in the last section meeting (attendance required). Slide decks are submitted for grading. Projects are graded on adherence to the six principles of Decision Quality. Software is optional — a complete decision analysis can be done on paper.

**Participation** is determined by attendance, voluntary contributions in class, and Poll Everywhere responses.

## Fulfillment of Requirements
