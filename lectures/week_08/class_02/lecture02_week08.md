# Ethics

### The question: Doing the *right* thing.  Is what we are doing *good*? 

why is this a problem?  Most glaringly because in our work we are just one member of a group, within society, with different values, taking different actions.  Is there "rationality" that is not just *individual* rationality? What about a choice that might lead to harm to others?

In this lecture we can only define the question -- there's a full course on "The Ethical Analyst" that follows this course. 

Our goal is, as practicing engineers (Decision Analysts) how do we devise a method that we can apply in our professional practice?  Think about it, and devise your own so that you have the ethical skills so that when situations arise you have your framework to fall back on. 

### What is the ethical content of DA?  
Lets start with some ethical concepts (the Stoic's virtues) -- how one should act in one's life (a broader concept than rules of right and wrong.) Concepts of wisdom )
- DQ six principles can be viewed as a guide -- what makes a "good" decision?
- But in comparison to common ethical categories DA as an ethical framework is incomplete. 
For sake of argument, consider how DA stacks up to these four "cardinal virtues".
- **Wisdom** (prudence). Thinking through the aspects of how to act, including longer term and indirect consequences. DA offers a principled basis for reasoning - DQ: correct logic, but not inclusive of how to bring in other people and parties to the analysis.
- **Courage** (commitment). Acting on what one determines is good. Having the will to realize what is good. DA removes regret via "clarity of action", so to remove any restraints on making the best choice. This can free up the will.  DQ: commitment
- **Moderation** (temperance). Separating emotion from reason. Matching one’s actions to the situation at hand.  DA quantifies the prospects of each alternative, creating precision to match actions to outcomes DQ ??
- **Justice** (fairness). Giving each person what is due them. Do no harm to others. In this area we need a way to advance beyond individual rationality. 

### What are useful ways of thinking about ethics?  What are known frameworks?

**Consequentialisim** (Utilitarianism)  The good is in the outcome. 
**Rule based methods** (Deontological ethics, Kant) The good is in the rule
**Virtue ethics** (Aristotle, Stoicism) The good is in the act. 

Can we dispense with the idea of "the good" and come up with a working framework?
